Because of the effects that phylogeny may have upon the extant phenotype of a given species, however, data from a wide range of species can rarely be considered to represent fully independent data points. Using modern comparative statistics, which incorporate phylogenetic information, we reanalysed their data set such that the assumption of independence was not violated. If Heffner and Masterton’s hypothesis is correct, then one would expect evidence of strong correlated evolution between corticospinal tract anatomy and digital dexterity once the effects of the phylogenetic relationships between the species in the data set have been removed. The results show that a distinct bias in the number of primate species sampled by Heffner and Masterton signiŞcantly affected their Şndings. Furthermore, once phylogeny has been taken into account, only the length of the corticospinal tract Şbres showed a signiŞcant relationship with two out of the four behaviours analysed, digital dexterity and hand–eye coordination. Based upon our results we recommend the use of modern comparative statistics for synthesising neural structure and behaviour, rather than examining structure–function relationships in an ahistorical context. It is also evident that there is a need for data on the length and depth of the corticospinal Şbres for a greater range of species so that the relationship between the corticospinal tract structure and motor behaviour for mammals as a whole can be more readily interpreted. © 1999 Elsevier Science B.V. They further suggested that the evolution of longer and deeper Şbres occurred early within the primate lineage and that it has culminated in the long, deep tracts observed in modern humans [32]. The concept of lack of independence of species as data points has recently become pervasive throughout the Şeld of comparative biology and has resulted in the development of a number of new statistical methods which incorporate phylogenetic information and thus remove the effects of non-independence [20,23,30]. De.spite the extensive use of these new methods in the analysis of ecology, evolution and comparative physiol.ogy, e.g. [1,15,19,22], they have yet to be used exten.sively in the examination of structure–function relationships in comparative neuroscience (for an ex.ception see [12]). Heffner and Masterton [32] did at.tempt to address this issue and incorporate some phylogenetic information into their analyses, but they did so by analysing their anatomical traits at ‘phyletic levels’. The use of ‘phyletic levels’, as performed by Heffner and Masterton [32], does not accurately reşect phylogenetic distance, but is rather an attempt to demonstrate how the corticospinal tract has evolved from so-called ‘basal’ taxa, such as marsupials, through to ‘higher level’ taxa (e.g. hominids). Even if these levels are taken as a progression index, there is little evidence to support the choice of levels as representative of ‘stages’ in human evolution (particularly the use of New World or platyrrhine primates as an intermediate between prosimians and Old World or catarrhine pri.mates). In addition, the phyletic levels used by Heffner and Masterton vary widely. That is, it begins with the sub-class level, then through two orders, a suborder, and three successive families to terminate in a single species, humans. Thus, even if this progression index is an accurate representation of phenotypes ancestral to the human lineage, the analysis of traits at such widely varying phylogenetic distances is not likely to produce correct results because it is not linear. Finally, by not incorporating relatedness between the species, indepen.dence of the data points is compromised, casting doubt upon conclusions regarding the relationship between the neural structure and behaviour. This is speciŞcally important for the Heffner and Masterton data set because it contains species which are differentially re.lated to one another (i.e. a wide variance in the degree of relatedness between individual species) which makes the data set as a whole particularly prone to the con.founding effects of phylogeny. A second problem with the data set, is that some groups are over-represented and others under-repre.sented in the sample. Thus, some of the relationships between the structures and digital dexterity may result from the polarisation of the relationship at either end of a spectrum without suitable representatives in be.tween. If we take the species from Heffner and Master.ton’s data set for which data on the depth and length of the corticospinal tract is known (Table 1) and plot it against digital dexterity (Fig. 1), it is clear that the primates are all found in a common area, distinct from the remaining 13 species (Table 1). Thus, without ac.counting for the effects of phylogeny and over sampling of primates as a whole, the relationship between the anatomy of the tract and digital dexterity is suspect. To resolve the relationships between the anatomy of the corticospinal tract and digital dexterity, we chose to reexamine the Heffner and Masterton data set [31,32] using modern comparative methods that incorporate phylogenetic information. We tested for the strength of the evolutionary relationship between the length and depth of the corticospinal tract and digital dexterity, as well as the possible effects of over-sampling of pri.mates. Also, to further examine the relationship be.tween the corticospinal tract and digital dexterity, we have incorporated data on other motor behaviours that require Şne control of the forelimb. Grillner [27] proposed that skilled limb movements, and thus digital dexterity, are derived from limb posi.tioning and grasping during arboreal locomotion. To test this theory regarding the evolution of reaching and grasping movements we examined the relationship be.tween corticospinal tract anatomy and arboreality. In a study of the amount of cortex devoted to the corticospinal tract in various mammals, Nudo and Masterton [49] related the relative amount of cortex to dexterity, hand–eye coordination, food preference, trophic level and foraging and nesting locations. This implies that these behaviours may also be related to the anatomy of the corticospinal tract itself. Based upon Nudo and Masterton’s [49] ecological variables, we also examined the relationship between the length and depth of the corticospinal tract, feeding behaviour (i.e. preda.tion) and hand–eye coordination. If digital dexterity, arboreality, predation and hand– eye coordination, are related to corticospinal tract de.velopment, then we would expect to Şnd evidence of strong correlated evolution between corticospinal tract length and depth and different motor behaviours once the data are made independent (i.e. the effects of phy.logeny are removed). Furthermore, if over sampling has not seriously affected the results by polarising the data, then accounting for phylogeny should have little effect upon the relationships between the anatomical and behavioural data. 2. The I statistics are calculated for each level and then plotted on a graph in order to determine where autocorrelation varies the most with taxon level or phylogenetic distance. The rank at which the Moran value is highest is taken as the point at which the greatest amount of phylogenetic correlation occurs [40]. A nested ANOVA or AN.COVA can be used instead of the Moran’s test, but the Moran’s test was chosen in this instance because it incorporates more phylogenetic information than an ANOVA:ANCOVA and thus is less prone to any po.tential source of error derived from non-independence [23]. Once the appropriate level has been determined, us.ing spatial autoregression techniques, the variation in the trait of interest can be divided into two compo.nents: a phylogenetic and an environmental compo.nent [10,23,30]. This is calculated through the use of a weighted matrix which encodes the phylogenetic re.latedness of the species being examined. Using this matrix, predicted values of the trait can be calculated and correlated with the observed values to yield the phylogenetic component of the variation. Thus, if the actual values of the trait are largely predicted by the matrix, then the amount of variation attributable to phylogeny is high. To perform both of these analyses, the phylogenetic autocorrelation (PA) program was used [40]. As branch lengths between such diverse taxa are uncer.tain (also see above) and estimating such relationships is fraught with its own set of complications, taxo.nomic ranks were used to calculate the degree of re.latedness of the species being examined. 2.2.2. Independent contrasts To examine the correlation between the anatomical and behavioural traits, we used Felsenstein’s [16] in.dependent contrasts approach to correct for the ef.fects of phylogeny. The method of independent contrasts compares pair-wise trait values at each node of the phylogeny, with each comparison (or contrast) scaled by an expected S.D. [21,23,30]. These devia.tions are derived from information on the branch lengths of the phylogeny and a model of evolutionary change. Once more, as branch lengths are unknown and the phylogeny of all of the species is incomplete (i.e. not every species was examined within every taxon or clade), a speciational model of evolutionary change was used [1,15,21]. That is, evolutionary change only occurs at speciation events and thus, each branch length was standardised to a value of 1. The phylogeny of the species was taken from a number of sources (Fig. 2) [48,56,63], but it should be noted that the relationships between the taxa are rea.sonably well established. The phylogeny was loaded into the comparative analysis of independent con.trasts (CAIC) program [58], as well as the data set, and the contrasts calculated. All of the traits were considered to be continuous, so the ‘crunch’ option was used as recommended by the authors [58]. This option in the program calculates positive contrasts as those dependent variables which vary in the same di.rection as the independent variable. Conversely, nega.tive contrasts are those dependent variables which vary in the opposite direction to the independent variable. In accordance with previous comparative studies on the corticospinal tract, the depth and length of corti.cospinal Şbres were considered to be both dependent and independent variables, depending on the compari.son being made. As the progressive evolution towards a more arboreal and predatory lifestyle is thought to affect the corticospinal tract anatomy [49], length and depth were dependent variables in these instances. Dexterity and hand–eye coordination were, however, examined as independent variables. This is due to the theory that long and deep Şbres are required in order for these behaviours to occur [4,31,32,49], as opposed to the occupation of a different ecological niche caus.ing the development of longer and deeper Şbres. The output was then regressed through the origin [21], and an F-test for signiŞcance was performed on each resulting regression line. 3. Results 3.1. Autocorrelation As there were no congeneric species in this com.parison, the shortest phylogenetic distance which could be examined was that of family, which ade.quately described each species as each one was a rep.resentative of a distinct sub-family or family. The depth of penetration of corticospinal Şbres did not, however, exhibit an autocorrelation pattern (i.e. high at low distances and progressively decreasing as phy.logenetic distance increases) which suggests that it has little phylogenetic afŞnity (Fig. 3a). The length of the corticospinal tract itself (Fig. 3b), as well as all four of the behavioural measures (Fig. 4), showed the highest amount of phylogenetic correlation at the or.der level. Subsequent analysis using autoregression to determine the amount of variation which can be at.tributed to phylogeny was performed on the length of the corticospinal tract and the behavioural measures (Table 2). Most of the r2 values were substantial (i.e. greater than 40%), although the amount of phyloge.netic variation in the diet measurement was only 1.5%. 3.2. Independent contrasts All four variables, with the effects of phylogeny removed, were compared against both the length and depth of the corticospinal tract Şbres (Figs. 5–7). Only two regression lines were statistically signiŞcant, that of dexterity and hand–eye coordination with the length of the corticospinal tract (Fig. 5b and Fig. 6c). Conversely, no signiŞcant relationship was observed between the depth of penetration of corticospinal tract Şbres for any of the four variables (Fig. 5aFig. 7), nor was any relationship observed between diet and arboreality and length of the corticospinal tract (Fig. 6a and b). Note that the relationships between depth and length of the corticospinal tract and dex.terity are quite different from that of the raw data (Fig. 1), once the effects of phylogeny are removed (Fig. 5). 4. Digital dexterity Based upon the superŞcial pattern of distribution of the length and depth of the tract and the degree of dexterity, Heffner and Masterton [31,32] proposed that the longer and deeper the Şbres of the corti.cospinal tract, the Şner the control of the digits and the greater the dexterity. Despite the signiŞcant rela.tionship between the length, and not the depth, of the tract and dexterity, it is important to acknowledge that the presently used dexterity index (based upon Napier [47]) has some serious şaws in accurately en.coding a species’ actual dexterity [33,35,36]. One ex.ample of this is the rat which Heffner and Masterton [31,32] gave a dexterity value of 3. It is clear from the work of Whishaw and colleagues, who have demonstrated that the rat is not only capable of sin.gle paw grasps with different paw postures [66], but also independent digit movement [67], that the rat should receive a ranking of 5 or 6. The same can be said of the chimpanzee which is clearly capable of a Şnely tuned precision grasp [11,46] and should there.fore receive a rank of 7 and not 6. The raccoon, however, which Heffner and Masterton tentatively gave a 3, was appropriately ranked, contrary to the common misconception that racoons possess a ‘pri.mate-like’ ability to handle objects [36]. The extent to which this index is incorrect for other species is not clear, but as more data are amassed on the ‘true’ dexterity of different species [33–36,69], it has become evident that a new index is required. A preliminary index based upon a multi-trait character matrix has been used in the examination of marsupial dexterity [34], but the extent to which this new index can be readily applied to other species has yet to be fully evaluated. The functional signiŞcance of the length of the corti.cospinal tract has yet to be fully explored. It is interest.ing, however, that even without the large amount of phylogenetic correlation for both dexterity and length of Şbres, there is still a signiŞcant relationship. Clearly, this is an area for further research with respect to how the length of the corticospinal tract results in a greater amount of dexterity and has interesting repercussions when considering the correlated evolution of the tract and digital dexterity (see below). Most of the research into digital dexterity has been primarily concerned with the depth of penetration of corticospinal Şbres. Arboreality The lack of a signiŞcant relationship between the corticospinal tract anatomy and degree of arboreality casts some doubt upon the hypothesis that a long and deep corticospinal tract is required for Şne control of the hand. Most arboreal mammals, and particularly those examined in this study, use their hands exten.sively in arboreal locomotion [9]. In fact, in some species, such as those moving around in the Şne branch domain (e.g. slow loris), one could make the claim that Şne motor control is required for effective arboreal locomotion. Thus, a lack of a signiŞcant relationship between arboreality and the corticospinal tract may suggest that the corticospinal tract is not essential for grasping per se, but for some other factor which is related to hand-use. Furthermore, Grillner’s theory [27] that skilled limb use is derived from arboreal locomo.tion does not appear to hold with respect to the anatomy of the corticospinal tract. 4.5. Hand–eye coordination As with the measurement of dexterity, this measure has its şaws and may not accurately represent the actual degree of hand–eye coordination exhibited by the species. This measurement, in particular, was not based upon any ‘real data’, rather it was an arbitrary score given to a set of various species by Nudo and Masterton [50] based upon whether the animals use vision to guide forelimb movements. Thus, any results based upon this index may be misconstrued by the subjectiveness of the original researchers. Nevertheless, a signiŞcant relationship was found between the length of the corticospinal tract and the hand–eye coordination index. This could suggest that the corticospinal tract is, in some way, involved in the coordination of forelimb movements and the guidance of the movements. Despite the problems as.sociated with this index, we feel that this is the most likely scenario: the development of the corticospinal tract is correlated with the degree of coordination re.quired to perform a task. This mode of evolution would account for the positive relationship between dexterity and hand–eye coordination and the length of the corticospinal tract as well as the lack of rela.tionship between arboreality and the corticospinal tract. 5. Conclusions Through the use of modern comparative techniques which take phylogenetic relationships into account, we have shown that previous comparative examina.tions of the corticospinal tract have been affected by a sampling bias in favour of primates. Despite this bias, once the effects of phylogeny have been re.moved, a signiŞcant relationship remains between the length of the corticospinal tract and digital dexterity and hand–eye coordination. Thus, the corticospinal tract can be thought of as evolving diversity in the length of its Şbres as a means of increasing or de.creasing the amount of motor coordination required in the forelimbs, but not necessarily for creating spe.ciŞc types of movement (e.g. independent digit move.ment). This hypothesis is supported by previous research into the function of the corticospinal tract as most, if not all, of the behavioural deŞcits reported following corticospinal tract lesions or sectioning (for a review see Ref. [54]) can be explained just as parsi.moniously if they are considered to be a lack of coor.dination between limb segments, rather than a lack of motor ability [68]. Future research into this phe.nomenon should be directed towards assessing motor coordination and motor function during different be.havioural tasks and comparing it to corticospinal tract length. The lack of relationship between the depth of pene.tration of the Şbres and any of the behavioural mea.sures examined remains puzzling. Why does such a diversity in the depth of penetration of the Şbres exist if there is no apparent connection between behaviour and depth? The best way to resolve the issue of the functionality of the corticospinal tract and how it re.lates to its anatomy is to broaden our knowledge of the diversity of the connections of the corticospinal tract by incorporating a more diverse array of spe.cies. Only at that point, can we begin to speculate on the evolution of the entire corticospinal tract within mammals as a whole. Acknowledgements Our thanks to Dr John L Gittleman for walking us through the use of modern comparative methods and to the two anonymous reviewers who greatly im.proved the quality of this paper.
